Understanding Key Stock Metrics

When comparing companies, certain financial metrics can help you understand their value and performance. Below, we've broken down some of the key metrics to help you make sense of them.

Market Capitalization (Market Cap)

Market Cap is the total value of a company's shares of stock. It is calculated by multiplying the stock price by the number of outstanding shares. A higher market cap indicates a larger, more established company.

Enterprise Value (EV)

Enterprise Value represents the total value of a company, including its debt. It is often seen as a more accurate measure of a company's worth than market cap because it considers both equity and debt. EV is the sum of the market cap and the company's debt, minus any cash.

Trailing P/E Ratio

The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio compares a company's current stock price to its earnings per share (EPS) over the past 12 months (trailing). A higher P/E suggests that investors are willing to pay more for each dollar of earnings, often due to expectations of future growth.

Forward P/E Ratio

The Forward P/E ratio is similar to the trailing P/E, but instead of looking at past earnings, it uses projected future earnings. This metric gives investors an idea of how expensive the stock might be relative to its expected earnings.

PEG Ratio

The Price/Earnings-to-Growth (PEG) ratio factors in a company's expected earnings growth, giving a more comprehensive view than the P/E ratio alone. A lower PEG ratio can indicate a stock is undervalued based on its growth prospects.

Price-to-Sales (P/S) Ratio

The Price-to-Sales ratio compares a company's stock price to its revenues. A lower P/S ratio may indicate that the stock is undervalued relative to its revenue. This is especially useful when a company has no earnings yet.

Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

The Price-to-Book ratio compares the company's stock price to its book value (the value of its assets minus liabilities). A lower P/B ratio might suggest that the stock is undervalued relative to its assets.

Enterprise Value to EBITDA

EV/EBITDA compares the company's Enterprise Value to its Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization (EBITDA). This ratio is often used to value companies with a lot of debt. A lower EV/EBITDA ratio suggests the company may be undervalued compared to its earnings potential.
